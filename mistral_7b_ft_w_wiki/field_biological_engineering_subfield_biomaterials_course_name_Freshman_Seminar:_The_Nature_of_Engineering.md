#### 1.2b Medieval Engineering

The Middle Ages, also known as the medieval period, was a time of significant technological advancements and engineering feats. This period, spanning from the 5th to the 15th century, saw the rise of feudalism, the Crusades, and the Renaissance. It was also a time of great innovation in engineering, particularly in the areas of architecture, fortification, and military technology.

##### Medieval Architecture

Medieval architecture was characterized by the use of Gothic and Romanesque styles. Gothic architecture, which emerged in the 12th century, was known for its pointed arches, ribbed vaults, and flying buttresses. These features allowed for larger and more light-filled structures, which were often used for religious purposes. The Notre-Dame Cathedral in Paris, built in the 12th and 13th centuries, is a prime example of Gothic architecture.

Romanesque architecture, on the other hand, was characterized by round arches, thick walls, and small windows. These features were designed to withstand the weight of the structure and to keep out the cold. The Abbey of Saint-Savin-sur-Gartempe, built in the 11th and 12th centuries, is a fine example of Romanesque architecture.

##### Medieval Fortification

Medieval engineers were also skilled in the art of fortification. Castles, which were built for defense and as residences for nobility, were designed to withstand siege. They were typically built on high ground, with thick walls, moats, and towers. The Tower of London, built in the 11th century, is a famous example of a medieval castle.

##### Medieval Military Technology

The Middle Ages was also a time of significant advancements in military technology. The crossbow, a powerful and accurate weapon, was developed during this period. The trebuchet, a catapult-like machine used to hurl large stones or other projectiles, was also invented during this time. These weapons were used in warfare, particularly in the Crusades and the Hundred Years' War.

In conclusion, the Middle Ages was a time of significant technological advancements and engineering feats. The skills and knowledge developed during this period laid the foundation for the modern field of engineering.

### Section: 3.1 Structural Properties of Trees:

Trees are complex structures that are constantly changing and adapting to their environment. In this section, we will explore the structural properties of trees, including their anatomy, growth patterns, and response to external forces.

#### 3.1a Anatomy of Trees

Trees are composed of various structural components, each with its own unique function. The main structural components of a tree include the trunk, branches, and leaves.

The trunk is the main stem of the tree, providing structural support and connecting all the other components of the tree. It is composed of xylem, which is responsible for transporting water and nutrients from the roots to the rest of the tree. The trunk also contains phloem, which is responsible for transporting sugars and other nutrients from the leaves to the rest of the tree.

Branches are extensions of the trunk, providing support and allowing the tree to reach out and capture more light. They are composed of both xylem and phloem, and are responsible for transporting water, nutrients, and sugars throughout the tree.

Leaves are the photosynthetic organs of the tree, responsible for converting sunlight into energy. They are composed of a thin layer of cells on the upper surface, known as the epidermis, and a thick layer of cells on the lower surface, known as the mesophyll. The epidermis is responsible for protecting the leaf from physical damage and water loss, while the mesophyll contains chloroplasts, which are responsible for photosynthesis.

#### 3.1b Growth Patterns of Trees

Trees grow in a specific pattern, known as the tree form. The tree form is determined by the arrangement of the branches and the shape of the crown. The most common tree forms are the pyramidal, conical, and spherical forms.

The pyramidal form is characterized by a single trunk and a pyramid-shaped crown. This form is commonly seen in coniferous trees, such as pines and spruces.

The conical form is similar to the pyramidal form, but with a more rounded crown. This form is commonly seen in deciduous trees, such as oaks and maples.

The spherical form is characterized by a rounded crown and multiple trunks. This form is commonly seen in fruit trees, such as apples and pears.

#### 3.1c Response of Trees to External Forces

Trees are constantly exposed to external forces, such as wind, rain, and snow. These forces can have a significant impact on the structural integrity of the tree.

Wind can cause trees to sway and bend, putting stress on the trunk and branches. This can lead to the formation of weak points, known as stress risers, which can increase the likelihood of branch failure.

Rain and snow can also cause stress on trees, particularly if they are not able to shed the water or snow quickly. This can lead to water or snow accumulation, which can cause the tree to become unstable and potentially fall over.

To mitigate the effects of these external forces, trees have developed various adaptations. For example, some trees have developed flexible trunks that can bend and sway in the wind, reducing the stress on the tree. Others have developed specialized structures, such as spikes or burls, that can help shed water and snow.

In addition to these adaptations, engineers also play a crucial role in helping trees withstand external forces. By designing and implementing structural support systems, such as guy wires or bracing, engineers can help stabilize trees and prevent them from falling over.

In conclusion, the structural properties of trees are essential for their survival and adaptation to their environment. By understanding these properties and how they respond to external forces, engineers can design and implement effective solutions to help trees thrive in a changing world.

### Subsection: 4.1a Basics of Acoustics

Acoustics is a fundamental aspect of musical instrument design and plays a crucial role in determining the sound quality of an instrument. It is the branch of physics that deals with the study of sound, including its production, transmission, and effects on the human body. In this section, we will explore the basics of acoustics, including the properties of sound and how they relate to musical instrument design.

#### Sound Production

Sound is a form of mechanical energy that is created by vibrations. When an object vibrates, it creates a wave that propagates through the medium surrounding it. This wave is what we perceive as sound. In the context of musical instruments, sound is produced by the vibration of strings, membranes, or other resonant bodies.

The frequency of the vibration determines the pitch of the sound. The higher the frequency, the higher the pitch. The amplitude of the vibration determines the loudness of the sound. The larger the amplitude, the louder the sound.

#### Sound Transmission

Sound travels through a medium, such as air or water, at a constant speed. The speed of sound is determined by the properties of the medium. In air at sea level, the speed of sound is approximately 343 meters per second.

Sound waves can be reflected, refracted, or absorbed by objects in their path. Reflection occurs when a sound wave bounces off an object. Refraction occurs when a sound wave changes direction as it passes through a boundary between two different media. Absorption occurs when a sound wave is absorbed by an object, converting its energy into heat.

#### Sound and Musical Instruments

The design of a musical instrument is largely determined by the principles of acoustics. The size, shape, and material of an instrument can greatly affect its sound. For example, a guitar with a larger body will produce a louder and more resonant sound than a guitar with a smaller body.

The strings of a guitar are under tension, which causes them to vibrate when plucked. The vibration of the strings creates a sound wave that propagates through the air. The size and shape of the guitar body affect how this sound wave is transmitted and amplified.

The sound hole in a guitar is an example of a sound port. Sound ports are used to control the direction of sound propagation. In a guitar, the sound hole is designed to project the sound outward, allowing the player and audience to hear the instrument.

### Subsection: 4.1b Sound Production in Instruments

#### Bowing

Bowing is a method of sound production in stringed instruments such as violins and cellos. In this method, a bow is drawn across the strings, causing them to vibrate. The vibration of the strings creates a sound.

The pitch of the sound produced by bowing a string is determined by its length, just like in plucking. However, the bow can also be used to vary the pitch by applying more or less pressure on the strings.

#### Blowing

Blowing is a method of sound production in wind instruments such as flutes, clarinets, and trumpets. In this method, air is blown into the instrument, causing the air column inside to vibrate. The vibration of the air column creates a sound.

The pitch of the sound produced by blowing into a wind instrument is determined by the length of the air column. The longer the air column, the lower the pitch. The diameter of the air column also affects the pitch, with larger diameters producing lower pitches.

#### 5.1a Basics of Scanning Electron Microscopy

The operation of the SEM is based on the principles of electron optics and electron beam interactions with matter. The electron beam is generated by a heated filament, similar to a cathode ray tube. The beam is then focused by a series of electromagnetic lenses onto the sample surface.

The electron beam is scanned across the sample surface in a raster pattern. As the beam interacts with the sample, it causes various phenomena such as secondary electrons, backscattered electrons, and characteristic X-rays to be emitted. These signals are detected by various detectors and are processed to create an image of the sample's surface.

The secondary electrons (SE) are low-energy electrons that are emitted from the sample surface due to the interaction with the primary electron beam. They provide information about the topography of the sample surface.

Backscattered electrons (BSE) are high-energy electrons that are scattered back from the sample due to the interaction with the primary beam. They provide information about the atomic number of the elements present in the sample.

Characteristic X-rays (CX) are emitted from the sample due to the interaction of the primary beam with the sample's atoms. They provide information about the chemical composition of the sample.

The signals are detected by various detectors and are processed to create an image of the sample's surface. The SE signals are used to create a topographic image, while the BSE and CX signals are used to create elemental maps.

The secondary electrons (SE) are low-energy electrons that are emitted from the sample surface due to the interaction with the primary electron beam. They provide information about the topography of the sample surface.

Characteristic X-rays (CX) are emitted from the sample due to the interaction of the primary beam with the sample's atoms. They provide information about the chemical composition of the sample.

The signals are detected by various detectors and are processed to create an image of the sample's surface. The SE signals are used to create a topographic image, while the BSE and CX signals are used to create elemental maps.
