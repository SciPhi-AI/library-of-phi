The sensitivity of AES to surface species arises from the fact that emitted electrons usually have energies ranging from 50 eV to 3 keV, resulting in a short mean free path in a solid. This means that the escape depth of electrons is localized to within a few nanometers of the target surface, giving AES an extreme sensitivity to surface species.

To prevent electron scattering off of residual gas atoms and the formation of a thin "gas (adsorbate) layer" on the surface of the specimen, most AES setups are run under ultra-high vacuum (UHV) conditions. This also allows for better analytical performance. A typical AES setup consists of focused electrons incident on a sample, with emitted electrons being deflected into a cylindrical mirror analyzer (CMA). The collected Auger electrons are then plotted as a function of energy against the broad secondary electron background spectrum.

Since the intensity of the Auger peaks may be small compared to the noise level of the background, AES is often run in a derivative mode that serves to highlight the peaks by modulating the electron collection current via a small applied AC voltage. This is achieved through the equation $\Delta V = k\sin(\omega t)$, where the collection current becomes a function of the applied voltage.

### Auger Electron Spectroscopy (AES)

AES is based on the principle that when an electron beam passes through a material, some of the electrons will lose energy due to interactions with the atoms in the material. This results in a spectrum of energy losses, which can be analyzed to determine the composition and properties of the material.

There are several different variants of AES, classified by the geometry and kinetic energy of the incident electrons. The most common variant is surface-sensitive AES, where the electrons are emitted from the top few nanometers of the sample. Other variants include bulk-sensitive AES, where the electrons are emitted from deeper within the sample, and scanning AES, where the beam is focused to a small spot on the sample.

One of the most powerful applications of AES is energy-filtered imaging. This technique involves selectively filtering the energy of the electron beam, allowing for the detection of specific elements or compounds in the sample. This is achieved by placing a thin film of a specific material in the path of the beam, which absorbs electrons with energies below a certain threshold. This allows for the visualization of specific elements or compounds in the sample, providing valuable information about their distribution and concentration.

## Experimental Setup and Quantification

### Instrumentation

XPS utilizes the photoelectric effect to analyze the surface of a material. A focused beam of x-rays is directed at the sample, causing the emission of photoelectrons from the surface. These photoelectrons are then collected and analyzed by a spectrometer, which measures their kinetic energy and intensity.

The kinetic energy of the photoelectrons is directly related to the binding energy of the electrons in the material, providing information about the chemical composition and electronic structure of the surface. The intensity of the photoelectrons is proportional to the number of atoms of a particular element present in the surface, allowing for quantitative analysis.

### Probing Inverted Ligand Fields

One of the most interesting applications of XPS is in the study of inverted ligand fields. Inverted ligand fields occur when the energy levels of the ligand orbitals are higher than those of the metal orbitals, resulting in a reversal of the expected d-orbital ordering. This phenomenon has been observed in various systems, including organometallic complexes and metal surfaces.

Computational methods have played a crucial role in understanding the nature of bonding in systems with inverted ligand fields. The Hoffman group has completed many calculations to probe the occurrence of inverted ligand fields in various systems. In a study of the absorption of CO on PtBi and PtBi2 surfaces, they found that the t<sub>2g</sub> metal orbitals were higher in energy than the e<sub>g</sub> orbitals, indicating the presence of an inverted ligand field. This was attributed to the bismuth-based ligands being higher in energy than the metal d orbitals.

The Mealli group developed the program Computer Aided Composition of Atomic Orbitals (CACAO) to provide visualized molecular orbital analyses based on perturbation theory principles. This program successfully displayed orbital energy inversion in organometallic complexes containing electronegative metals bound to electropositive ligand atoms. This bonding was described as a ligand-to-metal dative bond or sigma backdonation.

Alvarez and coworkers also used computational methods to illustrate ligand field inversion in various systems. In a study of Ag(III) salt KAgF<sub>4</sub>, they found that the Ag d orbitals were below those of the fluoride ligand orbitals, confirming the presence of an inverted ligand field.

Overall, XPS has proven to be a valuable tool in the study of inverted ligand fields, providing valuable insights into the nature of bonding in these systems. Its surface sensitivity and ability to measure electronic structure make it a powerful technique in electron microprobe analysis.

Cryo-preparation, also known as cryogenic sample preparation, is a technique used to preserve samples in their natural state at extremely low temperatures. This technique is particularly useful for biological samples, as it allows for the preservation of delicate structures and minimizes any potential damage caused by traditional sample preparation methods.

The process of cryo-preparation involves rapidly freezing the sample in a liquid ethane/propane solution, which is kept at a temperature of around -180C by a liquid nitrogen bath. This freezing process results in the formation of a vitrified ice, where water molecules do not have enough time to form hexagonal lattices as found in regular ice. This vitrified ice is similar to a glass-like state, preserving the sample in its natural form.

One of the main advantages of cryo-preparation is that it allows for the imaging of samples in their aqueous solution state, without the need for drying or staining. This is particularly beneficial for biological samples, as it avoids any potential perturbations caused by drying or staining agents.

However, there are also some limitations to cryo-preparation. One of the main challenges is achieving enough contrast to locate particles in the vitrified ice. This can make it difficult to resolve structures that are smaller than ~75 kilodaltons. Additionally, to attain atomic-level structure information, multiple images must be taken and averaged through a process called single-particle reconstruction, which can be computationally intensive.

Cryo-preparation is a relatively new technique, but it has already proven to be a valuable tool in electron microprobe analysis. Its ability to preserve samples in their natural state makes it a valuable addition to the advanced sample preparation techniques used in electron microprobe analysis.
