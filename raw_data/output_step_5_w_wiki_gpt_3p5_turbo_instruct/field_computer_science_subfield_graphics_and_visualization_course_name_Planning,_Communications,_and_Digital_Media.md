Balance is the state of equalized tension and equilibrium in a design. It is achieved by distributing elements evenly throughout the design, creating a sense of stability and order. Symmetrical balance involves mirroring elements on either side of a central axis, while asymmetrical balance involves distributing elements in a way that creates a sense of balance without being identical on both sides.

#### Hierarchy/Dominance/Emphasis

Hierarchy, dominance, and emphasis are all related principles that help guide the user's eye through a design. Hierarchy refers to the organization of elements in order of importance, with the most important elements being the most prominent. Dominance is created by using contrast in size, color, or positioning to draw attention to a specific element. Emphasis is the focal point of a design, which should be the most dominant element.

#### Scale/Proportion

Proportion, on the other hand, refers to the relationship between elements in terms of size. It is important to maintain a sense of proportion in a design to avoid a cluttered or unbalanced look.

#### Similarity and Contrast

Geographic Information Systems (GIS) is a powerful tool that has become increasingly important in various fields such as urban planning, environmental management, and emergency response. #### Components of GIS

GIS is composed of four main components: hardware, software, data, and people. The hardware component includes the physical devices used to collect, store, and process spatial data, such as computers, GPS devices, and scanners. The software component consists of the programs and applications used to manipulate and analyze the data, such as ArcGIS, QGIS, and Google Earth. The data component is the most crucial part of GIS, as it provides the information needed for analysis and visualization. Lastly, the people component refers to the individuals who use GIS to solve problems and make decisions.

#### Data Types in GIS

GIS deals with two types of data: spatial data and attribute data. Spatial data refers to the physical location and shape of objects on the Earth's surface, such as roads, buildings, and rivers. It can be represented in two forms: raster and vector. Raster data is composed of a grid of cells, with each cell containing a value that represents a specific attribute. On the other hand, vector data is composed of points, lines, and polygons that represent real-world features. Attribute data, also known as non-spatial data, provides additional information about the spatial data, such as the name, population, or elevation of a specific location.

#### Coordinate Systems in GIS

Coordinate systems are used to define the location of spatial data on the Earth's surface. There are two types of coordinate systems used in GIS: geographic coordinate systems (GCS) and projected coordinate systems (PCS). GCS uses latitude and longitude to define the location of a point on the Earth's surface, while PCS uses a grid system to represent locations on a flat surface. The choice of coordinate system depends on the type of data and the purpose of the analysis.

Object-based spatial databases are a type of GIS that uses a top-down approach to organize and store spatial data. #### C-squares

C-squares is an example of a grid-based approach used in GIS. It is a global grid that uses alphanumeric identifiers to represent cells on the Earth's surface. These identifiers are designed to be concordant with recognizable and commonly used intervals of latitude and longitude, making it easier to integrate data across national boundaries.

C-squares has several advantages, including the ability to represent data "footprints" for spatial search, data binning to reduce complex data into more manageable blocks, and a hierarchical approach where finer resolutions of the grid are nested into coarser ones. This hierarchical approach allows for a shared notation, making it easier to integrate data from different sources.

PPGIS is a method of using GIS to involve the public in the collection, analysis, and use of spatial data. It is based on the idea that the public has valuable knowledge and insights about their own communities and can contribute to the decision-making process through the use of GIS technology.

The bottom-up approach, on the other hand, involves working with the public to let them learn and use GIS technology to produce their own GIS data. This approach is more participatory and empowers the public to be actively involved in the decision-making process.

#### Approaches to PPGIS

There are two main approaches to PPGIS use and application: top-down and bottom-up. These two perspectives are currently debated in the PPGIS community.

The top-down approach to PPGIS was first envisioned as a means of mapping individuals by many social and economic demographic factors in order to analyze the spatial differences in access to social services. #### The Concentric Zone Model

The Concentric Zone Model, also known as the Burgess Model, was developed by sociologist Ernest Burgess in 1925. It is based on the idea that cities grow outward from a central business district (CBD) in a series of concentric rings. Each ring represents a different land use, with the CBD at the center and the residential areas at the outermost ring.

The model is divided into five zones:

1. The CBD - This is the central business district, where the city's main commercial and financial activities take place. It is characterized by high-rise buildings, offices, and retail stores.

2. The Zone of Transition - This is the second ring surrounding the CBD and is often referred to as the "inner city." It is a mixed-use area with a high concentration of low-income housing, factories, and warehouses.

3. The Working-Class Zone - This is the third ring and is where the majority of the city's working-class population resides. It is characterized by small, single-family homes and apartments.

4. The Residential Zone - This is the fourth ring and is where the middle-class population lives. It is characterized by larger, single-family homes and more spacious apartments.

5. The Commuter Zone - This is the outermost ring and is where the wealthier population resides. It is characterized by suburban neighborhoods and larger homes.

The Concentric Zone Model was based on observations of Chicago in the 1920s, but it has been applied to many other cities around the world. It has been criticized for oversimplifying the complex nature of cities and for not accounting for factors such as transportation and land use regulations. However, it remains a useful tool for understanding the general patterns of urban development and can be adapted to fit the unique characteristics of different cities.

#### Applications of the Concentric Zone Model

The Concentric Zone Model has been used by urban planners to guide the development of cities and to identify potential areas for growth and improvement. Additionally, the model has been used to study the social and economic dynamics of cities. For example, researchers have used the model to analyze patterns of segregation and inequality in urban areas. #### Limitations of the Concentric Zone Model

While the Concentric Zone Model has been a valuable tool for urban planners and researchers, it has its limitations. ### Section: 10.2 Transportation Models

Transportation planning involves the use of various models to analyze and predict the outcomes of different transportation strategies. These models help planners make informed decisions and develop effective plans to address transportation issues. In this section, we will discuss the four-step model, one of the most commonly used transportation models.

#### 10.2a Four-Step Model

The four-step model is a comprehensive approach to transportation planning that involves four main steps: trip generation, trip distribution, mode choice, and trip assignment. This model was first introduced in the 1950s and has since been refined and adapted to fit different transportation scenarios.

The first step in the four-step model is trip generation, which involves estimating the number of trips that will be made in a given area. This is done by analyzing data on population, employment, and land use. The output of this step is a trip table that shows the number of trips between different zones in the study area.

The second step is trip distribution, which determines the flow of trips between different zones. This step takes into account factors such as distance, travel time, and transportation modes. The output of this step is a trip distribution matrix that shows the number of trips between each origin-destination pair.

The third step is mode choice, which involves predicting the mode of transportation that individuals will use for their trips. This step takes into account factors such as cost, convenience, and availability of different modes of transportation. The output of this step is a mode choice matrix that shows the number of trips for each mode of transportation.

The final step is trip assignment, which determines the specific routes that individuals will take for their trips. This step takes into account factors such as road capacity, traffic flow, and travel time. The output of this step is a traffic assignment matrix that shows the number of trips on each route.

The four-step model is a useful tool for transportation planners as it allows them to analyze and predict the outcomes of different transportation strategies. However, it is important to note that this model has its limitations. It assumes that individuals make rational decisions based on cost and convenience, which may not always be the case. Additionally, it does not take into account the impact of external factors such as weather or accidents.

Despite its limitations, the four-step model remains a valuable tool in transportation planning. It provides a structured approach to analyzing and addressing transportation issues and allows for the evaluation of different scenarios and strategies. As technology and data continue to advance, the four-step model will likely continue to evolve and improve, making it an essential tool for transportation planners.

#### Subsection: 12.4 Gentrification and Displacement

Gentrification is a process that has been widely debated and studied in the field of housing and community development. It refers to the influx of wealthier residents into a previously low-income or working-class neighborhood, often resulting in the displacement of the existing residents. This phenomenon has both positive and negative effects on the community, and it is important to understand and address these impacts.

##### Effects of Gentrification

As mentioned in the related context, gentrification often brings visible changes to the infrastructure of a neighborhood. This can include the rehabilitation of old and deteriorated housing, the conversion of industrial buildings into housing, and the consolidation of multiple properties into single lots. These changes can improve the overall appearance and quality of the neighborhood, making it more attractive to potential residents and businesses.

However, the process of gentrification also has negative effects, particularly on the existing residents of the neighborhood. As wealthier residents move in, property values and rents tend to increase, making it difficult for low-income residents to afford to stay in their homes. This can lead to displacement, where residents are forced to move to other, often less desirable, areas. Displacement can also occur indirectly, as the rising cost of living in the neighborhood makes it difficult for small businesses and community organizations to survive, further eroding the sense of community and support for the existing residents.

##### Addressing Gentrification and Displacement

The issue of gentrification and displacement is a complex one, and there is no one-size-fits-all solution. However, there are some strategies that can be implemented to mitigate the negative effects and promote more equitable development.

One approach is to implement inclusionary zoning policies, which require a certain percentage of new housing developments to be affordable for low-income residents. This can help to maintain a diverse mix of residents in the neighborhood and prevent displacement. Additionally, community land trusts, where land is owned collectively by a nonprofit organization and leased to residents at affordable rates, can also help to preserve affordable housing in gentrifying areas.

It is also important for local governments to consider the needs and perspectives of existing residents when making decisions about development and revitalization in their neighborhoods. This can be achieved through community engagement and participatory planning processes, where residents have a say in the future of their community.

#### Definition and Purpose of Zoning

Zoning is a regulatory tool used by local governments to control the use of land and guide development in a particular area. It involves dividing land into zones, each with its own set of regulations for new development. #### Types of Zones

Zoning can be divided into three main types: single-use, mixed-use, and form-based. Single-use zoning, also known as Euclidean zoning, is the most common type and involves dividing land into zones for a single specific use, such as residential, commercial, or industrial. This type of zoning is based on the assumption that certain land uses are incompatible and should be separated to avoid potential conflicts.

Mixed-use zoning, on the other hand, allows for a combination of compatible land uses within a single zone. This type of zoning promotes a more diverse and walkable community by allowing for a mix of residential, commercial, and recreational activities in one area.

Form-based zoning focuses on the physical form and design of buildings rather than their specific use. This type of zoning aims to create a cohesive and aesthetically pleasing built environment by regulating the size, shape, and placement of buildings.

#### Benefits and Drawbacks of Zoning

Zoning can also be inflexible and may not always adapt well to changing circumstances and needs.

Some of the key principles of urban design include:

- Human Scale: Urban design should prioritize the needs and experiences of people over vehicles and buildings. This means creating spaces that are walkable, accessible, and comfortable for pedestrians.

- Mixed-Use Development: A mix of residential, commercial, and recreational uses should be integrated into the design of a community to create a vibrant and diverse environment.

- Connectivity: Urban design should prioritize connectivity between different parts of the community, whether it be through pedestrian-friendly streets, bike lanes, or public transportation.

- Sustainability: The design of a community should prioritize sustainability by incorporating green spaces, energy-efficient buildings, and sustainable transportation options.

- Sense of Place: Urban design should create a sense of place and identity for a community by incorporating local history, culture, and traditions into the design.

- Flexibility: The design of a community should be flexible and adaptable to changing needs and trends. This allows for the evolution and growth of a community over time.

### Section: 18.2 Evolution of Planning Theory

Planning theory has evolved significantly over the years, adapting to the changing needs and complexities of society. In this section, we will explore the evolution of planning theory and its impact on the field of digital media.

#### The Emergence of Rational Comprehensive Planning

One of the earliest and most influential planning theories is the Rational Comprehensive Planning model. Developed in the 1950s and 1960s, this model was based on the idea that planning should be a rational, scientific process that involves identifying and evaluating all possible alternatives before making a decision. It assumes that planners have access to all necessary information and can make objective, rational decisions based on that information.

#### Criticisms and Limitations of Rational Comprehensive Planning

While the Rational Comprehensive Planning model was innovative at its conception, it has faced significant criticism and has fallen out of mass use in recent years. One of the main criticisms is that it is based on unrealistic assumptions, such as the availability of complete and accurate information. In reality, planners often have to work with limited information and make decisions under uncertainty. Additionally, the model does not take into account the political and social context in which planning takes place, leading to potentially flawed decisions.

#### Alternatives to Rational Comprehensive Planning

In response to the limitations of the Rational Comprehensive Planning model, alternative planning theories have emerged. One such theory is the Incremental Planning Model, which suggests that planning should be a continuous, adaptive process that involves making small, incremental changes rather than large, sweeping decisions. This model recognizes the complexity and uncertainty of planning and emphasizes the importance of flexibility and adaptability.

#### Planning in the Digital Age

#### Planning for Resilience

As we look towards the future of planning, it is crucial to consider the concept of resilience. Resilience is the ability of a system or community to withstand and recover from shocks and stresses, whether they be natural disasters, economic downturns, or social disruptions. In the face of an increasingly uncertain and complex world, planning for resilience is becoming more and more important.

One way to approach planning for resilience is through the framework of multi-layer defense. This framework categorizes risk mitigation measures into three layers of defense: prevention, response, and resilience. Prevention involves taking proactive measures to prevent a disaster from occurring, such as building stronger infrastructure to withstand natural disasters. Response involves the actions taken during and immediately after a disaster to minimize its impact. And resilience involves the ability to bounce back and recover from a disaster, whether it be through community support systems or adaptive strategies.

In order to effectively plan for resilience, it is crucial to consider a wide range of potential risks and threats. This includes not only natural disasters, but also human-caused disasters such as pandemics, cyber attacks, and global catastrophic risks. These risks are often interconnected and can have cascading effects, making it even more important to plan for resilience at multiple levels.

Funding is also a crucial aspect of planning for resilience. Unfortunately, research and initiatives relating to existential risks are often underfunded. This is despite the fact that these risks have the potential to cause catastrophic harm to humanity. As we look towards the future, it is important to allocate more resources towards understanding and mitigating these risks.

Survival planning is another approach to building resilience in the face of global disasters. This involves the establishment of self-sufficient, remote settlements specifically designed to withstand and survive a disaster. While this may seem like a drastic measure, economist Robin Hanson argues that even a small refuge housing 100 people could greatly improve the chances of human survival during a global catastrophe.

In addition to these strategies, technology will also play a crucial role in planning for resilience. The rise of smart cities, which use technology and data to optimize services and improve quality of life, has the potential to greatly improve the efficiency and effectiveness of planning processes. Virtual and augmented reality technologies can also aid in the visualization and communication of planning proposals, as well as data analysis and visualization.

In conclusion, planning for resilience is essential for the future of planning. By considering a multi-layer defense approach, addressing a wide range of potential risks, and utilizing technology, we can build more resilient communities and systems that are better equipped to withstand and recover from disasters. As we continue to face an uncertain and complex future, planning for resilience will be crucial in ensuring the safety and well-being of our communities.
