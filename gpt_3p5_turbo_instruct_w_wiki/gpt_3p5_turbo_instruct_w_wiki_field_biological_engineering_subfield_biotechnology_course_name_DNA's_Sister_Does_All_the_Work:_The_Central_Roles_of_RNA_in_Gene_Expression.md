## Chapter 2: The Journey of RNA:



### Section: 2.1 From the discovery of mRNA to the 'central dogma':



RNA, or ribonucleic acid, is a crucial molecule that plays a vital role in the journey of gene expression. It was first discovered in 1868 by Friedrich Miescher, who isolated it from the nuclei of white blood cells. However, it was not until the 1950s that scientists began to understand the true significance of RNA in gene expression.



In 1956, scientists Elliot Volkin and Lawrence Astrachan discovered that RNA plays a crucial role in protein synthesis. They found that when the bacteriophage T2 infects Escherichia coli (E. coli) bacteria, it causes a rapid turnover of RNA. This led to the discovery of messenger RNA (mRNA), which carries the genetic information from DNA to the ribosomes for protein synthesis.



This discovery was a breakthrough in understanding the process of gene expression and led to the formulation of the 'central dogma' of molecular biology. Proposed by Francis Crick in 1958, the central dogma states that genetic information flows from DNA to RNA to proteins. This results in a mature mRNA molecule that contains only the genetic information needed to create a protein.



The process of RNA processing is crucial for the proper functioning of cells. siRNAs, or small interfering RNAs, are short double-stranded RNA molecules that play a crucial role in gene silencing. The mechanism of siRNA-mediated gene silencing involves a series of steps that ultimately lead to the degradation of the target mRNA molecule. First, the siRNA molecule binds to a protein complex called the RNA-induced silencing complex (RISC). The RISC then unwinds the siRNA molecule, exposing one of its strands, known as the guide strand.



The guide strand then binds to the target mRNA molecule, which is complementary to it. This binding triggers the cleavage of the mRNA molecule by the RISC, leading to its degradation. As a result, the target gene is silenced, and the production of the corresponding protein is prevented.

#### mRNA: The Blueprint for Proteins

The process of creating mRNA from DNA is known as transcription. During transcription, the enzyme RNA polymerase binds to a specific region of DNA called the promoter and begins to unwind the double helix. It then reads the DNA sequence and creates a complementary mRNA strand by adding nucleotides one by one. This process continues until the RNA polymerase reaches a specific sequence of nucleotides called the terminator, where it detaches from the DNA and releases the newly formed mRNA strand.



The mRNA strand then travels out of the nucleus and into the cytoplasm, where it binds to ribosomes. The ribosomes then read the mRNA sequence and use it as a blueprint to assemble amino acids in the correct order, forming a polypeptide chain, which will eventually fold into a functional protein.



#### rRNA: The Building Blocks of Ribosomes



Ribosomal RNA (rRNA) is the second type of RNA involved in protein synthesis. It is found in the ribosomes, which are the cellular organelles responsible for protein synthesis. rRNA makes up the majority of the ribosome's structure and plays a crucial role in the process of protein synthesis. It helps to align the mRNA and tRNA molecules and catalyze the formation of peptide bonds between amino acids, ultimately leading to the creation of a polypeptide chain.



#### tRNA: The Transporter of Amino Acids



Transfer RNA (tRNA) is the third type of RNA involved in protein synthesis. It is responsible for transporting amino acids to the ribosomes, where they are assembled into proteins. tRNA molecules have a specific three-dimensional structure that allows them to bind to a specific amino acid on one end and a specific sequence of nucleotides on the other end. This sequence of nucleotides, called the anticodon, is complementary to the codon on the mRNA strand. This ensures that the correct amino acid is added to the growing polypeptide chain, according to the genetic code.

#### 20.1a Subtopic 1: Introduction to RNA



In the previous chapters, we have learned about the structure and function of DNA, the molecule that stores genetic information. However, DNA alone cannot carry out the process of gene expression. This is where RNA, or ribonucleic acid, comes into play. RNA is a single-stranded molecule that is involved in various cellular processes, including gene expression.



Similar to DNA, RNA is made up of nucleotides, but it contains the sugar ribose instead of deoxyribose. Additionally, RNA contains the nitrogenous base uracil instead of thymine, which is found in DNA. There are three main types of RNA: messenger RNA (mRNA), ribosomal RNA (rRNA), and transfer RNA (tRNA). Each type of RNA plays a unique role in the process of gene expression.



### Subsection: 20.1b Subtopic 2: Messenger RNA (mRNA)



Messenger RNA, or mRNA, is responsible for carrying the genetic information from DNA to the ribosomes, where proteins are synthesized. This process is known as transcription. During transcription, the DNA sequence is copied into a complementary mRNA sequence, with the exception of thymine being replaced by uracil.



Once the mRNA is transcribed, it undergoes a process called RNA processing, where non-coding regions, known as introns, are removed and the remaining coding regions, known as exons, are spliced together. This ensures that the final mRNA sequence contains only the necessary information for protein synthesis.



### Subsection: 20.1c Subtopic 3: Ribosomal RNA (rRNA)



Ribosomal RNA, or rRNA, is a structural component of ribosomes, the organelles responsible for protein synthesis. rRNA makes up the majority of the ribosome and plays a crucial role in the translation of mRNA into proteins. It helps to align the mRNA and tRNA molecules and catalyzes the formation of peptide bonds between amino acids.



### Subsection: 20.1d Subtopic 4: Transfer RNA (tRNA)



Transfer RNA, or tRNA, is responsible for bringing amino acids to the ribosome during protein synthesis. tRNA molecules have a specific sequence of nucleotides that correspond to a specific amino acid. This allows tRNA to recognize and bind to the correct amino acid and deliver it to the ribosome.



### Subsection: 20.1e Subtopic 5: The Central Dogma of Molecular Biology



The central dogma of molecular biology is a fundamental principle that explains the flow of genetic information in living organisms. It states that DNA is transcribed into mRNA, which is then translated into proteins. This process is essential for the proper functioning of cells and is regulated by various mechanisms to ensure the accurate expression of genes.

#### 20.1d Subtopic 4: Transfer RNA (tRNA)



Transfer RNA, or tRNA, is a small RNA molecule that plays a crucial role in protein synthesis. It acts as a carrier molecule, bringing the correct amino acid to the ribosome during translation. tRNA molecules have a specific sequence of nucleotides that correspond to a specific amino acid. This sequence is known as the anticodon.



During translation, tRNA molecules bind to the mRNA sequence at the ribosome, using their anticodon to match with the codon on the mRNA. This ensures that the correct amino acid is added to the growing polypeptide chain. Once the amino acid is added, the tRNA is released and can be reused for another round of translation.



tRNA molecules also play a role in the accuracy of protein synthesis. They have a proofreading mechanism that helps to ensure that the correct amino acid is added to the polypeptide chain. This helps to prevent errors in protein structure and function.



In addition to their role in protein synthesis, tRNA molecules also play a role in regulating gene expression. Some tRNA molecules have been found to have regulatory functions, such as controlling the translation of specific mRNAs or regulating the activity of enzymes involved in gene expression.



Overall, tRNA plays a crucial role in the process of gene expression, ensuring that the correct amino acids are added to the growing polypeptide chain and regulating gene expression. Without tRNA, the process of protein synthesis would not be possible, and the proper functioning of cells would be compromised. 





### Section: 20.1 Subtopic 7: The Role of RNA in Gene Expression



#### 20.1e Subtopic 5: Ribosomal RNA (rRNA)



Ribosomal RNA, or rRNA, is a type of RNA that plays a crucial role in protein synthesis. It is a major component of ribosomes, the cellular organelles responsible for translating mRNA into proteins. rRNA makes up about 80% of the ribosome's mass and is essential for its structure and function.



There are three types of rRNA: 5S, 16S, and 23S. These numbers refer to the sedimentation coefficients of the rRNA molecules, which is a measure of their size. The 16S rRNA is found in the small subunit of the ribosome, while the 23S and 5S rRNA are found in the large subunit.



The primary function of rRNA is to provide a structural framework for the ribosome and to catalyze the formation of peptide bonds between amino acids during translation. The rRNA molecules have specific binding sites for tRNA and mRNA, ensuring that the correct amino acids are added to the growing polypeptide chain.



In addition to its role in protein synthesis, rRNA also plays a role in the regulation of gene expression. Recent studies have shown that rRNA can act as a regulatory molecule, controlling the translation of specific mRNAs and influencing the activity of enzymes involved in gene expression.



One of the most interesting aspects of rRNA is its high degree of conservation across species. The 16S rRNA, in particular, has a highly conserved sequence, making it a useful tool for studying evolutionary relationships between organisms. This conservation also highlights the importance of rRNA in the process of gene expression, as any changes to its sequence could have significant impacts on protein synthesis.



In conclusion, rRNA is a crucial component of the ribosome and plays a vital role in protein synthesis. Its conservation across species and regulatory functions make it a fascinating molecule to study in the context of gene expression. Without rRNA, the process of translation would not be possible, and the proper functioning of cells would be compromised.
